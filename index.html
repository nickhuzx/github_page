Warning for Cataholics

Dangerous Type of Drug Sweeps the World

A most funny phenomenon in modern society is that, we walk dogs on the road, but we post about cats on the Internet.

Once you are connected to the Internet, you will find yourself bombarded with pictures and videos of cats. This fluffy animal has been playing an increasingly important role and displaying its existence more than ever.

In the UK, the average number of cat pictures people post on the Internet a day is 3,400,000, almost triples that of selfies. The craziness for cat is also the same in China.

Among the discussion about keeping cats and dogs in the famous Chinese Q&A community Zhihu, discussion associated with cats accounts for over 70 percent.

Also, in the past year, a public account on Wechat named “We are Cataholics”, came first among tens of thousands of public accounts under the category of “Lohas”, with its startling reading amount reaching 210,000,000 times. To put it in another way, almost 16 percent of Chinese people have read articles from this Cataholic account once in the past year.

Cataholics exist all over time, but it seems that the craziness for cat suddenly becomes so overwhelming, that if you own a cat, it means that you own the whole world. It’s like a new form of addictive drug, a drug that modern people cannot live without.

What does cat bring us?

“C” for cuteness, cure, and companionship.

A year ago, Elaine never expected becoming one of the most envied type of people on social media, one who owns a cat.

She didn’t even know how this craziness for cat suddenly bombarded the whole Internet, but did realize why she was so willing to nurse this fluffy master, “Bubble is sooooo cute!”

Bubble is a Garfield cat with a flat, round face, exactly the same with “The Garfield” except its grayish-brown fur coat. He is not an outgoing clingy cat, and likes to hide himself in Elaine’s bedroom.

“How can you restrain yourself from holding this fluffy cute angel,” Elaine exclaimed, “Their innocent faces will definitely shed your armor down and turn you into a loyal fan.”

It is natural for Elaine to post about Bubble on Instagram every day. Bubble has become an important part of her life.

“He is so silly but cute! Envy me!” Elaine wrote on her recent post, with great satisfaction.

It’s true that cats’ cute appearances attract people, but there is more to this crazy “cat addiction”. If you ask a Cataholic why he or she cannot live without cats, nine out of ten will answer that it is actually the companionship and cure cat brings that they cannot live without.

In reality, what Bubble is to Elaine goes far beyond being a major character on Instagram.

Elaine had never thought that someday she would have a cat. She even had never raised any fluffy animal before having Bubble, and her only experience about keeping pets was having a turtle and a goldfish that stayed in the fish tank all the time.

“But having Bubble is definitely different. So different.” Elaine said.

Growing up in Guangdong and studying in Hong Kong for a Master’s degree, Elaine gradually got tired of the familiar land and decided to come to Beijing for work, but things weren’t the same as expected.

For a long period of time, Elaine hated staying in the empty apartment after work, and didn’t come home until late. Fortunately, Bubble’s existence illuminates Elaine’s life.

“Before Bubble arrived, this apartment was only where I live. But now, this is home.”

Bubble looks silly because of his flat, round face, but Elaine thinks that he is actually clever and keen. Whenever she is in a low mood, Bubble will definitely notice that, and heal Elaine in his own way.

There was a period of time when Elaine was at the crossroads of life, and could not sleep very well at night. When Bubble heard that Elaine was turning over, he would climb up onto her bed and lie right beside her, asking her to scrub his fur.

“This means a lot to me. He becomes family, a kind of companionship.”

When people have their own cat, they tend to be totally open and frank to their cat, displaying their fragile side, which has been hidden for a long time from other human beings.

This kind of frankness was also rewarded. Cats play with you and rely on you, displaying all their cuteness and innocence only to you, which is the best and the most exclusive form of reward.

Unlike Elaine, Xueshan, who is still a college student, does not have the luck to keep a cat at dorm, but it cannot stop her from regarding cats as an important source of emotional support. When Xueshan found that there were actually homeless cats on campus, she felt that she discovered great treasure.

Xueshan especially loves the cat with snow-white fur, and often deliberately passes by the road where it often appears. Meeting and touching “Snow-white” can “help sustain hours of studying”, and in the past month, there were 15 posts about cats in her Wechat Moments, mostly about Snow-white.

However, for those who cannot raise cats in real life due to various reasons, or meet homeless cats like Xueshan, watching cat videos or playing cat-raising games on the Internet becomes a good alternative.

When seeing these cute little creatures dressing up like dolls and suddenly move their faces close to the camera, the stress and exhaustion of a long day’s work will sure disappear.

Yuri is a genuine member of the “Internet Cataholics”. Preparing for the national entrance examination for postgraduates, Yuri was under great pressure, and cats then become her exclusive cheerleader. “Seeing pictures and videos of this fluffy little creature helps me a lot. They are little healers.”

A cat a day keeps the doctor away. This is definitely true for Cataholics in modern society. Cats are new form of drugs, providing people an exit to get away from the tedious life, and sink themselves into the “planet of cuteness”.

It’s hard to describe this healing power of cats in concrete words, but its overwhelming effect is evident, just like how Elaine was dragged out of sadness when Bubble jumped onto her bed and scrubbed against her arm, how Snow-white Xueshan met acted as a good listener when she was in low mood, and how Yuri escaped from the stress of daily life through watching cat videos on her phone before sleep.

So what does cat bring us? Their cute appearance, and more importantly the cure and companionship they provide, might be the answer. Whether through interacting with real cats, or becoming an Internet Cataholic, there is no denying that cats are becoming an increasingly important source of cure for modern life.

Who are Cataholics, and why do we love cats?

The secrets lie behind our time

The love for cat is not new. Archaeological findings show that the history between human and cats dates back to more than 9,000 years ago.

In China, the earliest record about cats was in the Book of Songs, written 2,000 years ago. The large number of folk songs and poems about cats show that they were important and unique even in early people’s life.

Lu You, a famous Chinese poet, was also lost in the charm of cat, expressing his love in many of his poems. In Song Dynasty, cat-keeping reached a historical peak, and cat food market even emerged.

The love for cat has lasted for more than 2,000 years in China, and sure will never disappear. However, to describe the relationship between the new generation of Cataholics and their “soulmate-cat”, “love” wouldn’t be the right word. Instead, they are fanatical, aspirational, obsessive, and addictive to cats.

The craziness for cats takes root in modern life.

It’s not difficult to sketch out this new generation of Cataholics — mostly in their twenties or thirties, well-educated but struggling in big cities, single and alone, dreaming about a perfect and successful life. As a result, the cuteness, cure and companionship cat brings can act as a good remedy to save them out of the tedious life.

Dogs are also cute, and have long been loyal friends of human beings, but why Cataholics are all over the Internet and the society, displaying a rather dominating position?

To be a bit extreme, Cataholics are somewhat “products of our time.” To describe the features of the new generation of Cataholics, a startling truth is that, it is closely related with what we hate, what we fear, and what we desire.

Unlike dogs, cats stand for the unbounded and free nature of self, and that’s what modern people desire most.

“Sometimes I feel that being a cat is great,” Ziqin joked. Born in the 1990s under the One-Child Policy, Ziqin was used to living alone without brothers or sisters. She was described as “professional” and “independent” in work, but only herself knew that it was not easy to deal with all kinds of relationships in work, and in daily life.

However, cats are free from the complicated ties and relationships that constrain modern people. For Ziqin, getting along well with colleagues, roommates, and family members takes much effort, but in her eyes, cats are living a totally different life. It seems that they have seen through everything, therefore cares about nothing.

“Cats are only living for themselves,” Ziqin concluded, “but it is impossible for most people to follow”. As a young woman in career, it might be better for Ziqin to restrain personal characteristics.

It’s hard for a grown-up to cut off all ties and say that “I care about nothing”, but that’s what every cat do. They do not flatter anyone for food or for love, and sometimes even ignore their hosts’ calls. “This is what I envy most,” Ziqin sighed.

Besides, the unbounded and independent nature of cats also suits the fast-pace life of modern people well. For the new generation of Cataholics, cats bring the necessary companionship and warmth without disturbing their work and independence, or adding pressure to the “already stressful” life.

Elaine describes her relationship with Bubble as “interdependent, but independent”. This kind of relationship could never exist if Bubble were a dog.

Working in a well-known Internet Company in Beijing, Elaine has a lot to deal with every day, and could not spare much time for Bubble. As a result, Bubble’s independent character suits Elaine’s busy calendar perfectly. It just takes Elaine a few minutes to prepare food and shovel Bubble’s poop before work every day, and she knows that Bubble will take good care of himself after she leaves.

“But if you have a dog, you will feel very guilty if you do not take him out for a walk.” Elaine said.

When Elaine was doing her work at home, Bubble would always lie beside her laptop quietly, without disturbing. This is the unique form of companionship cat provides, a kind of companionship that leaves each other within his or her cozy boundary, but at the same time displays silent love and support.

“I do not feel bounded by this kind of relationship,” Elaine added, “It’s even an ideal kind of relationship between lovers.”

For Internet Cataholics, the bind is even lesser. They do not need to feed the cat, take it to the pet clinic, or shovel the poop every day, but can see these cute little creatures on social media and thus feel satisfied and lightened.

The unbounded relationship prevents all possible troubles that may lead to negative emotions, and suits modern people well, who are under too much pressure and need a way to vent their feelings.

Cherie once kept a cat for her friend for about two weeks. “Cats are definitely angels,” Cherie said, “When they sit on your sofa, you feel that you need nothing more.”

Two weeks later, when Cherie had to say goodbye to this little angel, she felt that she lost a big part of her life. She wrote on Wechat, “A person who does not have a cat is like wild grasses”.

Cherie’s short experience with cats sank her into the charm of cat, and she soon became an Internet Cataholic, watching cats’ videos and downloading cat pictures regularly on her phone. The pleasure of actual interaction is less, but it does help avoid potential sadness, like saying goodbye.

“There’s already too much pressure in life,” Cherie sighed, “and I cannot afford saying goodbye again.”

Becoming an Internet Cataholic is a good alternative for people like Cherie, who needs the cure from cats to relieve pressure, but also want to avoid excessive emotional costs. And because it suits so well the fast-pace life of young people who are struggling for success, that it has actually became a popular subculture on the Internet.

We are what we love. People become Cataholics because they could see traces of themselves from cats, and they tend to cast their own image onto the unbounded creature.

Cataholics are more introverted in general, and prefer to stay alone instead of socializing with others. The findings of Facebook Research proved this point. Data from 160,000 users showed that cat owners like to stay at home to watch movies or read books. Dog owners, by contrast, are more outgoing, with 26 more friends on average than cat owners.

Lacking or hating actual social contact, Cataholics find that the Internet is a good alternative to fulfill their need to connect without creating much complicated ties, and at the same time realizing an ideal kind of connection based on common interest, which is the love for cats.

With an anonymous ID that covers the real identity, Cataholics are finally set free on the Interent.

A major way for Cataholics to connect with others and express themselves on the Internet is through producing and using “cat expression packages”.

“Wang Huang Maori” is a representative celebrity cat which became famous because of its facial expressions. Pictures of this ginger cat, who was born with a sad face, have become a popular source of cat expression packages on social media.

Maori’s inverted triangle eyes make him stand out in a unique way. Even if you do not know his name, you definitely have seen his disheartened face matched with unconstrained annotations as popular expression packages, which greatly fits the prevailing disheartened culture in modern society.

Cats actually have very few facial nerves, thus many of their facial expressions do not have real meanings. However, Cataholics tend to cast their own emotions onto cats, trying to provide an explanation or understanding for cats’ facial expressions in the form of words.

This self-casting and self-mapping of human emotions onto cats greatly promotes the production of countless number of cat expression packages from Cataholics. Also, because expressions of the cats together with the words could explain perfectly the emotions of human beings, more and more people who are originally not fan of cats start using these expression packages, which again contributes to the popularity of the Cataholic subculture.

How does cat change the society?

The rise of “cat economy”

The Cataholic subculture has led to a remarkable growth of cat owners, and money spent on cats has also increased substantially, covering cat food, medical treatments, health products, toys and treats.

According to Ali Data, in 2017, cat owners in China spent 8.7 billion yuan altogether on Taobao, the biggest e-commerce website in China. The average expense was 302 yuan. In general, cat food and cat litter is the main expenditure.

The post-80s spent 357 yuan for their cats each month on average, being the highest among all age groups.

Each month, Elaine spends nearly 300 yuan for Bubble. Besides normal cat food and cans, she sometimes feed Bubble with nutria-vita to ensure his health. However, the cat litter, food bowl and toys bought for Bubble are generally moderate in price.

Different from Elaine, a growing number of cat owners tend to provide high-end pet products for their ‘masters’. Thus, more brands are emerging to meet customers’ needs.

These brands claim to provide high-quality life for pets based on technology. Petkit, known for its sterile food bowl and smart water dispenser, is a representative one.

The sales of the water dispenser have reached 4000 each month despite its price, with the cheapest one being close to 300 yuan.

The comments of this product may reveal Cataholics’ mentality. “I don’t have to worry about the water quality for my cat anymore.” For Cataholics, cats are not simply animals, but deserve equality with human beings, like the need for high quality water.

Noticing the huge potential of this market, even some famous household brands begin to target Cataholics’ wallets.

The well-known Swedish brand IKEA has already launched the pet furniture series, “Lurvig”, in the United States and Japan. Created by “pet loving designers”, as the official website described, this series is specially designed to keep the four-legged “family members” comfortable and occupied, including cat houses, slow-feed bowls, scratching mats and so on.

Besides the influence on entity economy, the craziness for cats on social media has also led to the appearance of Fan Economy among Internet Cataholics. Celebrity cats on social media create new profitable opportunities.

Cat bloggers might initially want to share their daily life with cats, but fans soon accumulated to such a large number that drew the attention of advertisers.

The most well-known pet blogger in China, “Hui Yi Zhuan Yong Xiaomajia”, has successfully made a packet by sharing his life with his cat and dog on Weibo.

So far, more than 29 million people have followed Xiaomajia, outnumbering the population of Shanghai.

This huge fan base led him to an offer from a professional Blogger company, providing him with comprehensive marketing strategies. Soon after, Xiaomajia began to sell calendars, books and other derivative products related to his cat.

The loyalty of his fans builds up gradually as time goes on. Currently, Xiaomajia posts various kinds of advertisements, including products that have nothing to do with his cat, like cars, and e-commerce website. By posting each piece of advertisement, Xiaomajia earns 20,000 yuan, which is almost three times the monthly salary of ordinary office workers in cities like Beijing or Shanghai.

For most Internet Cataholics, derivative products of cats exert great attractions that they cannot deny. As a grown-up, Xueshan still bought herself coats with cat pictures on. “My dorm is too small. Every time I see these things, I have to try hard to stop myself from taking them all home.”

The Cataholic culture and the derived creative industries are far from saturation. Elements of cats can be grafted on almost everything. And more importantly, the majority of Cataholics are quite willing to pay for these fluffy friends who have brought them so much cure and warmth.

If getting a pillow with your favorite kitty on it can meet your psychological needs, then why not pay for it?

Most cat bloggers share similar path with Xiaomajia. Once they have accumulated a certain number of fans by posting cute or funny pictures of their cats, they would be verified by Microblog as “famous pet blogger”. This guarantees more advertising opportunities.

Besides professional pet bloggers like Xiaomajia, there are also bloggers, who were originally in other fields, became famous because of their cats.

Guo Site was originally a cartoonist on Weibo, and the protagonist of her comic works is taken from her Ragdoll “Fox”.

Sharing about her cats on social media frequently, Guo Site is considered by many of her fans as a pet blogger. Her two cats, Fox and Bun, win her more loyal fans than the comic series.

Posts associated with Fox or Bun generally have more than 2,000 pieces of reposts and comments, and short videos like taking a bath for cats would gain around 5,000 pieces of interactions on average. If taking “likes” into account, the number may generally exceed 10,000. This scale of interaction has exceeded that of most celebrities’ Weibo accounts.

In the Cataholic era, cat brings fortune and fame. The craziness for cats has turned cat blogger into a profitable career, creating new forms of industries related to cats.

Besides Weibo, cat economy is also active on other platforms like Wechat. There are many cat-themed public accounts, and “Here comes cat” is one of the most popular ones, which has attracted 500,000 fans in less than two years.

“Here comes cat” profits mainly on advertisements, but also launches other forms of activities to build fan loyalty, like online cat-raising games. Pillows, umbrellas and other manga-based products are also sold offline to improve additional value of the brand. Currently, they are working on paid content like “cat-raising classroom” designed for cat owners.

The team of “Here comes cat” has clearly recognized the niche market introduced by the Cataholic subculture. Focusing on e-commerce and paid content, “Here comes cat” aims at producing a unique symbol of the Cataholic subculture, and expanding the love of cat to a larger range.

Besides Weibo and Wechat, social media like Instagram and vertical websites like Youtube, Bilibili, Zhihu are also popular platforms, gathering a large number of Internet Cataholics to watch cat videos and interact with other Cataholics in their own way.

The Cataholic subculture have exerted its influence on mobile games as well. Cat-raising games like “The backyard of kitties” became a great success, exceeding 190,000,000 times of downloads.

Recently, the VR edition of this game has been adapted into a film, which will sure become another milestone for the Cataholic subculture.

“Once you become a Cataholic, you will always be one.” The charm of cats is like drugs, providing modern people with an exit to get away from all kinds of stress, and sink into the planet of cuteness.

Cats are becoming masters indeed, exerting its influence in all sectors, and controlling the emotions of human beings.

Are cats a dangerous type of drug? Will cats finally turn human beings into their minions? This is not for Cataholics to answer. It might be better to just seize the moment, and enjoy the glimmer of light cats bring to the dim life.
